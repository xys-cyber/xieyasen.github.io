2. Court Opinion Elements

Case Information

Case Name: Brown et al. v. Board of Education of Topeka et al.

Citation: 347 U.S. 483 (1954)

Court: Supreme Court of the United States

Argued: December 9, 1952; Reargued: December 8, 1953

Decided: May 17, 1954

Key Legal Elements

Appeal from the United States District Court for the District of Kansas. The case was consolidated with similar cases from South Carolina, Virginia, and Delaware.

  • Original filing: 1951
  • District Court decision: 1951
  • Supreme Court argument: 1952
  • Reargument ordered: 1953
  • Final decision: 1954

Black children were denied admission to public schools attended by white children under laws requiring or permitting racial segregation. The plaintiffs alleged that segregation deprived them of equal protection under the Fourteenth Amendment.

Key Fact: In each case, minors of the Negro race sought admission to public schools on a non-segregated basis.

Does racial segregation in public schools, even when physical facilities and other tangible factors are equal, deprive minority children of equal educational opportunities in violation of the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment?

YES. Racial segregation in public education is unconstitutional because separate educational facilities are inherently unequal.

"We conclude that in the field of public education the doctrine of 'separate but equal' has no place. Separate educational facilities are inherently unequal."

Majority Opinion by Chief Justice Warren:

  1. Historical Analysis: The history of the Fourteenth Amendment is inconclusive regarding segregation in education.
  2. Importance of Education: Education is perhaps the most important function of state and local governments in modern society.
  3. Psychological Impact: Segregation generates a feeling of inferiority that affects children's motivation to learn.
  4. Inherent Inequality: Separate educational facilities are inherently unequal, even if tangible factors are equal.

This landmark decision overturned Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) and its "separate but equal" doctrine, marking a pivotal moment in the American civil rights movement.

  • Declared racial segregation in public schools unconstitutional
  • Applied strict scrutiny to racial classifications
  • Led to desegregation efforts nationwide
  • Paved the way for broader civil rights reforms
